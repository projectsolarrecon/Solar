## Local Authority / Preemption Analysis

**Core local-authority finding:** Florida’s FDLE FAQ states that Florida registration laws do not themselves prohibit offenders/predators from living in certain areas or sharing a residence with another registrant, but that municipal and/or county ordinances may address where people can live, who they can or cannot contact, and areas they can or cannot be near or visit. FDLE also separates those local ordinances from supervision restrictions set by a judge, DOC, or DJJ. This means local authority must be treated as a real daily-life layer, not a generic caveat.

Local ordinances are city/county/municipal law. The V2 page should keep those overlays in separate callouts.

# Topic File

## 1. State posture summary

**Topic posture:** Reporting deadline-heavy  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  

**Controlling rule:**  
Florida operates a strict, public, deadline-heavy registry system. The packet identifies stacked duties: sheriff registration, FDLE registry duties, driver-license / ID duties, re-registration, travel reporting, transient reporting, internet identifier reporting, vehicle reporting, and public listing.

**Practical meaning:**  
Florida is not a state where someone can safely rely on memory, informal advice, or a single annual check-in. Many obligations use a 48-hour deadline. Missing a separate step — especially the DMV step or an in-state travel residence update — can create criminal exposure even if the person believed they had already “registered.”

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Nearly all adult registrants; especially people moving into Florida, moving within Florida, traveling within Florida, changing online identifiers, changing vehicles, or experiencing unstable housing.

**What changes the answer:**  
Sexual offender vs sexual predator status; offense type; victim age; transient status; supervision; local ordinances; July 1, 2026 effective-date changes.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S1, FL-S2, FL-S3, FL-S4, FL-S8, FL-S9, FL-S10, FL-S11.

**Confidence:** High  

**Validation gaps:**  
Local ordinances / county-city overlays; tribal-case specifics; supervision terms; implementation guidance for Ch. 2026-17 after July 1, 2026.

**V2 implications:**  
Florida’s `startHere` should lead with: strict deadlines, sheriff first, DMV second, public listing, and separate warnings for local ordinances and supervision/court/facility overlays.

## 2. Who must register

**Topic posture:** Broad statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Not applicable  

**Controlling rule:**  
Florida registration applies to people convicted of listed Florida offenses, qualifying similar offenses from other jurisdictions, and some people designated or required to register elsewhere who establish residence in Florida. Federal and military convictions are included in the conviction framework. Separate juvenile statutes govern some adjudicated youth.

**Practical meaning:**  
A person cannot assume that moving to Florida avoids registration because the conviction occurred elsewhere or because another state labels the case differently. Florida looks at offense comparability, qualifying designations, and required registration status.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Most adult convictions that match Florida’s listed offenses; many out-of-state, federal, and military cases; some juvenile cases.

**What changes the answer:**  
Exact offense; age of victim; adult conviction vs juvenile adjudication; out-of-state designation; whether the person is required to register in another jurisdiction; federal or military conviction status.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S1, FL-S2, FL-S6, FL-S7, FL-S9.

**Confidence:** High for adult, federal, military, and comparable-jurisdiction cases; Medium for tribal-case specifics.

**Validation gaps:**  
Tribal-specific treatment should be validated before publication.

**V2 implications:**  
Common question: “Do I have to register in Florida if my case was from another state?” Answer should be strong: assume yes-risk until Florida comparability is checked.

## 3. Initial registration deadlines

**Topic posture:** Reporting deadline-heavy  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Partially allowed  

**Controlling rule:**  
A sexual offender must report in person to the sheriff within 48 hours after establishing permanent, temporary, or transient residence in Florida. After sheriff registration, the person must report to a driver-license office within another 48 hours unless already processed through a qualifying custodial/DOC pathway. DOC-supervised nonincarcerated registrants have separate DOC registration timing, and local jails have intake/release registration duties.

**Practical meaning:**  
Initial registration is not one step. The practical Florida sequence is: sheriff registration first, then FLHSMV credential step. Someone who reports to the sheriff but fails to update the Florida driver license or ID may still be noncompliant.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Nearly all people establishing residence in Florida with a qualifying registration history.

**What changes the answer:**  
Whether the person is coming from custody, DOC supervision, local jail, another state, federal custody, or already has a Florida credential.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S1, FL-S6, FL-S8, FL-S9.

**Confidence:** High  

**Validation gaps:**  
Local sheriff offices administer registration intake and may vary in appointment process, form handling, locations, and required proof. The statewide deadline remains controlling.

**V2 implications:**  
Checklist must separate “registered with sheriff” from “completed Florida ID / driver-license step.”

Nearly all registrants.

## 5. Verification / re-registration schedule

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Not applicable  

**Controlling rule:**  
Florida sexual offenders generally re-register during the month of birth and again in the sixth month after the birth month. Certain sexual offenders convicted of more serious listed offenses re-register quarterly: during the birth month and every third month thereafter. Sexual predators re-register quarterly.

**Practical meaning:**  
Not everyone has the same schedule. Some people report twice yearly; others report four times yearly. The user must know whether their offense or designation places them in the quarterly group.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
All registrants, but cadence differs by designation and offense.

**What changes the answer:**  
Sexual predator designation; offense type; statutory offense list; birth month.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S1, FL-S2, FL-S9.

**Confidence:** High  

**Validation gaps:**  
The public page should carefully identify which offense list triggers quarterly sexual-offender reporting.

**V2 implications:**  
The output GPT should not say “Florida registrants report twice a year” as a blanket statement. It must say: twice yearly for many offenders, quarterly for predators and certain offenders.

## 6. Residence restrictions

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Allowed  

**Controlling rule:**  
Florida has a statewide 1,000-foot residence restriction, but it is not universal. It applies to people convicted of specified offenses where the victim was under 16. If a person already lives in a compliant residence and a school, park, child-care facility, or covered place is later established nearby, the person cannot be forced to move for that reason alone under the statewide rule. FDLE also states that municipal and/or county ordinances may outline where registrants can live.

**Practical meaning:**  
Florida does have a major statewide housing restriction, but the correct answer is not “all registrants cannot live within 1,000 feet.” The right answer is: some registrants — based on offense and victim age — are subject to the statewide 1,000-foot rule, and local rules may add restrictions that make an otherwise state-compliant address unusable.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Some registrants, especially those with specified offenses involving victims under 16. Many more may be affected by local housing ordinances, supervision residence approval, housing-provider rules, or private lease restrictions.

**What changes the answer:**  
Offense statute; victim age; date and location of residence; whether a protected site was created after the person already lived there; local ordinance; supervision/court order; housing-provider policy.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S3, FL-S9, FL-S15.

**Confidence:** High for statewide rule and FDLE local-authority warning; Medium for specific local ordinance scope.

**Validation gaps:**  
County/city ordinance sweep required before publication.

**V2 implications:**  
Housing section must avoid a false universal statement. It should say: “Florida has a statewide 1,000-foot rule for certain people, and local rules may be stricter.”

## 7. Presence / place restrictions

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONDITIONAL RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Allowed  

**Controlling rule:**  
The packet did not identify a current blanket statewide “no parks / no beaches / no pools / no libraries / no churches” rule for all registrants. Current statewide place restrictions are narrower, often tied to designation, offense, supervision, or specific conduct. FDLE points to Fla. Stat. § 856.022 and § 775.21(10)(b) for park/school/child-care questions and separately warns that municipal/county ordinances may address areas registrants can or cannot be near or visit. Ch. 2026-17 is enacted but not effective until July 1, 2026, and will expand child-location / public-pool / public-bathing-place related restrictions for specified people and offenses.

**Practical meaning:**  
Current Florida law should not be summarized as “all registrants are banned from public places.” But local ordinances, supervision, court orders, facility policies, and the July 1, 2026 law can change the practical answer substantially.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Current statewide law: some / certain people.  
Local ordinances: potentially many people depending on city/county.  
Future law: specified people/offenses after July 1, 2026.  
Supervision/court/facility overlays: case-specific.

**What changes the answer:**  
Offense type; victim age; sexual predator status; supervision; court orders; local ordinances; facility/event policy; whether the date is before or after July 1, 2026.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S2, FL-S9, FL-S11, FL-S14, FL-S15.

**Confidence:** Medium-High  

**Validation gaps:**  
Need final implementation validation for Ch. 2026-17 after effective date; local ordinances must be checked; facility policies and supervision orders must be kept separate from municipal law.

**V2 implications:**  
Create a dated update banner: “Florida enacted changes effective July 1, 2026. Do not apply those rules before the effective date.”

## 8. Employment restrictions

## 14. Removal / termination / reclassification

**Topic posture:** Removal narrow  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Not applicable  

**Controlling rule:**  
Florida’s special-circumstances removal statute allows relief only for limited offense categories and strict eligibility requirements, including victim-age and age-gap conditions, no disqualifying offenses, and federal compatibility concerns. FDLE also summarizes a separate long-term petition path for certain sexual offenders who have been lawfully released from sanctions, confinement, or supervision for at least 25 years and meet statutory conditions. Sexual predators have no ordinary petition path to remove the predator designation under FDLE’s FAQ.

**Practical meaning:**  
These are real legal pathways for narrow groups, not general off-ramps. Most Florida registrants should not expect removal unless their facts fit the statute closely.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Some / certain people only.

**What changes the answer:**  
Exact offense; victim age; age gap; number/type of convictions; adult vs juvenile status; length of time since release; arrest-free period; federal SORNA compatibility; judicial ruling; predator designation.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
FL-S1, FL-S2, FL-S5, FL-S9.

**Confidence:** High  

**Validation gaps:**  
Actual motion practice, court venue, current forms, and current case law should be validated before generating public step-by-step instructions.

**V2 implications:**  
Removal FAQ should be blunt: “Possible for a narrow group; not realistic for most people.”
