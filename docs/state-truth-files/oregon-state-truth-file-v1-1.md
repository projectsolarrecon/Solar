## Local Authority / Preemption Analysis

**Core local-authority finding:**  
The Oregon packet validates a strong statewide registration/classification system, but it does **not** validate a broad express preemption rule that either clearly allows or clearly bars local governments from adding registry-related residence, presence, event, facility, or public-place restrictions. Oregon’s statewide system itself is more targeted than Florida’s: public posting is level/designation-based; residence restrictions validated in this packet are tied to certain Level III / SVDO / supervised contexts; and child-location restrictions under ORS 163.476 apply to specific higher-risk/status categories, not all registrants. Because no Oregon-specific local preemption source was validated, the local-authority posture should be **“Unclear — validate”** for most local ordinance topics rather than assuming local governments are broadly allowed or barred.

**Separation rule for downstream output:**  
Local government rules are city/county/municipal ordinances or local public-facility rules. They are not the same thing as probation, parole, post-prison supervision, Board/PSRB conditions, treatment rules, court orders, custody orders, school policies, employer rules, housing-provider rules, shelter policies, or private lease terms. The V2 page should keep these categories separate.

## 1. State posture summary

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Unclear — validate  

**Controlling rule:**  
Oregon has a statewide registration and classification system under ORS Chapter 163A. Its practical posture is materially different from Florida: Oregon has recurring reporting duties and criminal penalties for noncompliance, but public posting is level/designation-based, relief and reclassification pathways are meaningful for some people, and the packet did not identify a universal statewide residence or place ban for all registrants. Oregon State Police confirms that not all people convicted of a sex offense, and not all juveniles adjudicated of a sex offense, are required to register in Oregon.

**Practical meaning:**  
Oregon is still a compliance system with deadlines, forms, annual reporting, address-change reporting, higher-education reporting, international-travel reporting, fees, and penalties. But the top-line page should not imply that every registrant is publicly posted, banned from living near children, or barred from parks/schools/churches/libraries. The sharpest statewide restrictions appear concentrated around Level III, sexually violent dangerous offender, dangerous-offender, supervision, and similar out-of-jurisdiction designations.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
All people required to report in Oregon; higher-consequence rules especially affect Level III, sexually violent dangerous offenders, people under supervision, nonresident workers/students, and people seeking relief or reclassification.

**What changes the answer:**  
Exact offense; adult conviction vs juvenile adjudication; Oregon vs out-of-state case; risk classification level; sexually violent dangerous offender status; supervision status; local authority if validated; school/work location; public website eligibility; relief/reclassification status.

**Confidence:** High for statewide architecture; Medium-Low for local authority/preemption until validated.

**Validation gaps:**

## 2. Who must register

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Not applicable  

**Controlling rule:**  
Oregon requires registration for people who meet the statutory definition of “sex offender” based on Oregon registerable sex crimes, comparable out-of-state/federal/military convictions or findings, and certain juvenile adjudications. Oregon State Police emphasizes that not all people convicted of a sex offense, and not all juveniles adjudicated of a sex offense, are required to register in Oregon.

**Practical meaning:**  
Oregon is not an “every sex-related conviction automatically registers” state. The correct practical question is whether the conviction, adjudication, finding, or out-of-jurisdiction case fits Oregon’s statutory registerable categories and reporting rules.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Many adult sex-crime convictions; some out-of-state, federal, military, and juvenile cases; nonresident workers and students under certain conditions.

**What changes the answer:**  
Offense statute; Oregon comparability; age/adjudication posture; whether the person resides, works, volunteers, or attends school in Oregon; whether the person is relieved from reporting elsewhere; juvenile court findings.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
OR-S1, OR-S2, OR-S3, OR-S4, OR-S11, OR-S12.

**Confidence:** High for Oregon and general out-of-state/federal/military comparability; Medium for tribal-specific edge cases.

**Validation gaps:**  
Tribal cases and complex out-of-state comparability should be validated before publication.

**V2 implications:**  
Common question: “Do all sex offenses require registration in Oregon?” Answer should be: “No. Oregon uses statutory registerable categories; verify the exact offense and case posture.”

## 3. Initial registration deadlines

**Topic posture:** Reporting deadline-heavy  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Partially allowed  

**Controlling rule:**  
Oregon’s official agency summary identifies key initial deadlines: Oregon residents generally report within 10 days after discharge, release, parole, post-prison supervision, or other supervised/conditional release; people moving to Oregon report no later than 10 days after moving; nonresidents attending school in Oregon report no later than 10 days after the first day of school attendance; and nonresidents employed or volunteering in Oregon report no later than 10 days after the 14th day of employment or vocation in Oregon. OSP also says registrants report in person to a local law-enforcement agency in the county where they reside.

**Practical meaning:**  
Oregon is less “48-hour heavy” than Florida but still deadline-heavy. A person moving, starting school, or working/volunteering in Oregon should count days carefully and should not wait for an annual check-in.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Oregon residents released into the community; people moving into Oregon; nonresident students; nonresident workers and volunteers.

**What changes the answer:**  
Resident vs nonresident status; release source; probation/court placement; moving date; school attendance date; 14th day of work/volunteering; juvenile adjudication; supervision instructions.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
OR-S3, OR-S4, OR-S20.

**Confidence:** High  

**Validation gaps:**  
Local registering-agency intake procedures and exact proof/document requirements should be checked before public step-by-step instructions.

**V2 implications:**  
Oregon checklist should use “10 days” as the main statewide deadline anchor, with special nonresident school/work rules.

## 6. Residence restrictions

**Topic posture:** Conditional statewide rule  
**Rule status:** CONDITIONAL RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Unclear — validate  

**Controlling rule:**  
The packet did not identify a blanket statewide Oregon residence-distance restriction for all registrants. Oregon’s validated statewide residence framework is narrower and tied to certain sex offenders, especially sexually violent dangerous offenders and Level III offenders under ORS 144.641–144.646, with DOC/community-corrections rules for permanent residence requirements during parole/post-prison supervision. Those rules include a general prohibition against allowing covered offenders to reside near locations where children are the primary occupants or users, exceptions, and restrictions on dwelling with another supervised sex offender unless authorized. Transitional housing is treated differently in the statute and rules.

**Practical meaning:**  
Oregon should not be summarized as “all registrants cannot live within X feet.” The better practical answer is: many registrants do not appear to face a blanket statewide distance rule, but Level III / SVDO / supervised people can face serious residence restrictions through supervision and DOC/community-corrections rules. Local authority to add broader residence restrictions was not validated in the packet and should not be assumed.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
Some / certain people: Level III offenders, sexually violent dangerous offenders, dangerous-offender/supervision categories, and people on parole/post-prison supervision. Local rules remain a validation issue, not a confirmed overlay.

**What changes the answer:**  
Risk level; SVDO designation; parole/post-prison supervision; residence approval; whether children are primary occupants/users nearby; transitional housing; local authority if validated; household composition.

**Source keys / official sources:**  
OR-S14, OR-S17, OR-S20, OR-S21, OR-S22.

**Confidence:** High for no universal statewide distance rule found in packet; Medium for state supervised-release framework; Low-Medium for local authority/preemption.

## 11. Public registry visibility

**Topic posture:** Public posting level/designation-based  
**Rule status:** CONFIRMED RULE  
**Local authority posture:** Not applicable  

**Controlling rule:**  
Oregon public release is tied to classification. Level III offenders are posted on the Oregon State Police public website unless an exception applies, including PSRB-supervision-related limits. Level II information may be released to specified people/entities such as household members, significant relationships, neighbors, churches, parks, schools, child care centers, businesses, and long-term care/residential care facilities. Level I information is more limited, generally to a person residing with the registrant.

**Practical meaning:**  
Oregon is not a broad public-posting state in the same way Florida is. Public visibility turns heavily on classification level. A Level I person may still have reporting duties but may not be publicly searchable in the same way a Level III person is.

**Scope / who is affected:**  
All registrants, but public visibility differs sharply by Level I, Level II, Level III, PSRB status, and information-release decisions.

**What changes the answer:**

# Contradictions or Near-Contradictions Detected

## 1. Registration vs public posting  
**Near-contradiction:** A person may be required to register but not appear on the public website.  
**Resolution:** Keep registration and public posting separate. Oregon public posting is level/designation-based, especially Level III.

## 2. “No blanket statewide place ban” vs Level III/SVDO child-location crime  
**Near-contradiction:** Oregon does not appear to ban all registrants from child-focused places, but ORS 163.476 imposes a serious restriction for Level III/SVDO/dangerous-offender categories.  
**Resolution:** State the scope precisely: not all registrants, but certain classifications/designations.

## 3. “No blanket residence rule” vs supervised residence criteria  
**Near-contradiction:** Oregon lacks a validated universal distance rule for all registrants, but supervised Level III/SVDO categories may face residence restrictions near child-occupied/user locations.  
**Resolution:** Treat housing as “no blanket statewide rule for all” plus “supervision/status-based statewide criteria.”

## 4. Meaningful relief vs continuing registration  
**Near-contradiction:** Oregon registration can continue indefinitely unless relieved, but relief/reclassification is meaningful for some people.  
**Resolution:** State both: registration continues until relief, but Oregon has real eligibility pathways.

## 5. Public website vs community notification  
**Near-contradiction:** Level II may not be publicly posted like Level III, but information may still be released to neighbors, churches, parks, schools, child care centers, businesses, and other entities.  
**Resolution:** Do not equate “not on public website” with “private.”

## 6. Local authority vs statewide rule  
**Near-contradiction:** The STF says no blanket statewide residence/place rule was found for all registrants, but local governments might still attempt to regulate housing or public-place access.  
**Resolution:** Do not assume local authority. Mark local authority/preemption as unresolved until a local-law validation pass is completed.

## 7. Local authority vs supervision/court-order overlays  
**Near-contradiction:** Residence/place access can be affected by local ordinances, supervision conditions, court orders, and facility policies, but those are legally different layers.  
**Resolution:** Keep them separate in every V2 output section.

## 8. No statewide rule vs local preemption  
**Near-contradiction:** “No statewide rule found” does not automatically mean local governments can regulate the topic.  
**Resolution:** When no statewide rule is found, state whether local authority is allowed, barred, unclear, or not applicable. For Oregon, most local authority remains “Unclear — validate.”

## 9. No statewide rule vs allowed local restrictions  
**Near-contradiction:** If future validation finds Oregon local restrictions are allowed, a “no statewide rule” answer may still be misleading for daily life.  
**Resolution:** Update topic posture after local validation; do not use local uncertainty as a default caveat now.

## 10. Transitional housing exception vs residence restrictions  
**Near-contradiction:** State rules restrict permanent residence for certain people near child-primary locations, but transitional housing is treated differently.  
**Resolution:** Keep permanent housing and transitional housing separate.
